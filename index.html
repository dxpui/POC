Understand

Illegal harms

You must assess the risk of each kind of priority illegal harm occurring on your service. These are the most serious offences as defined by Parliament, and all services will need to act to prevent users encountering content amounting to one of these offences. Ofcom has grouped all the priority offences into 16 kinds of illegal harm*.

Recommendation

We recommend that services assess the risks of harm from CSAM and Grooming separately. If you are a user-to-user service, you should consider the risks of image-based CSAM and of CSAM URLs separately. We will provide you with further help in assessing these risks later in the tool.

Ofcom’s Register of Risks provides more information on understanding risks. You should read the relevant sections of the Register of Risks to understand how offences may occur on your service.

Priority illegal harms

The harms that we have highlighted are the key types of illegal harm that are most relevant to the risk factors that you identified in Step 1. This will help you prioritise the most relevant parts of our Register of Risk that we encourage services to consult. This provides more detailed information to help you understand how harms may manifest on your service.

Terrorism offences

Terrorism is considered a violent action or threat of action, designed to influence a government and advance a cause. Online terrorism content is any content made available to others online, which can encourage or promote terrorism.

Child Sexual Exploitation and Abuse (CSEA) offences

CSEA involves forcing or enticing a child or young person to take part in sexual activities, not necessarily involving a high level of violence, whether or not the child is aware of what is happening. The activities may involve physical contact or non-contact activities. Sexual abuse can take place online, and technology can be used to facilitate offline abuse.

Grooming

Online grooming for child sexual abuse is the method of contacting children and developing a relationship, whether through flattery, emotional connection, sexualisation, bribery, blackmail or coercion, for the purposes of conducting child sexual abuse.

Child Sexual Abuse Material (CSAM)

Online CSAM is material which depicts penetrative sexual activity, non-penetrative sexual activity, or other indecent or prohibited imagery of children.

Encouraging or assisting suicide (or attempted suicide) or serious self-harm offences

This offence takes place when an individual intentionally encourages or assists a person to self-harm or end their life.

What you must consider in your risk assessment

When completing your risk assessment, the Online Safety Act requires you to assess each kind of priority illegal harm occurring on your service. Specifically, you must consider the risk of:

  • illegal content appearing on your service. ‘Illegal content’ means content that amounts to an offence. For example, encouraging users to support a proscribed organisation would be considered an offence (e.g. a terrorist group).
  • your service being used to commit a priority offence, through a functionality of your service. For example, a marketplace service could be used to commit an offence if it is possible for firearms to be sold through the service without appropriate checks.
  • your service being used to facilitate a priority offence. It is important to note that the content or behaviour which takes place on the service may not necessarily be illegal. For example, a messaging service that enables adults to contact children they do not know. It is not unlawful for an adult to contact a child they do not know, but this may facilitate the adult abusing the child. The use of a service to facilitate a crime may not generate illegal content, but it may lead to a risk of harm.
