Some examples include:

  • Dedicated pornography services, including those who host user-generated pornographic content
  • Discussion forums or chat rooms where suicide, self-harm, eating disorders, gore and violence or abusive and hateful content are the primary subjects of discussion.

In making this determination, services may find it helpful to consult our Guidance on Content Harmful to Children which provides examples of content which Ofcom would, and would not, consider to be CHC.

Based on your answers to the previous questions, you do not ban one or more kinds of content harmful to children and there is a higher risk of these kinds of content being shared on your service. This means that you are in scope of safety measures which require implementing age assurance. These measures allow you to know which of your users are children, and help you ensure that children are protected from harm and receive age-appropriate experiences online.

The principal purpose of your service will determine which type of age assurance measures apply to you. The aim of establishing the principal purpose is to determine whether children could realistically access and enjoy the service without encountering harmful content. We consider this to be unlikely for services dedicated to this content, where the risk of children being exposed to CHC is almost certain, and therefore there are no realistic alternative ways to manage this risk other than implementing HEAA to prevent users from accessing the entire service, unless they have been determined to be adults.

For services that do not ban CHC but it is not the principal purpose or main activity of the service, we recommend using HEAA to prevent or protect children from encountering specific kinds of content on your service in order to comply with the children’s safety duties.
